the art of getting knowledge

Everyone wants to be successful in their career and their field. Most beginners lack the knowledge about how things work, how they are planned, and how they are structured. As for me, I’m also in the same boat. I love doing conceptual work; it doesn’t matter if it's coding, maths, science, or even theoretical subjects. I want to have a deep dive into their core concepts and the perspective of what the author/ writer/ teacher wants to express. In my college curriculum, I read this lesson about Competency-based education. CBE is an approach to learning in which the focus shifts from the hours spent in the class to the mastery of skills. After reading more about this approach, I figured out where I’m lagging, what I have to change to get the maximum result and ROI on the time spent on the learnings, knowledge, and lectures in the class. Then I began to get more and more knowledge, I even began to write down what I was learning, so that I would never lose the understanding of the concepts which are directly linked to the precious time spent in understanding them. While learning the deep concepts, they felt like deep for me, as I was learning them alone at my own pace, even I’m thinking that I was doing something which is different from what others are doing. Then my favourite mentor told me about the Illusion of Competence The “Illusion of Competence” is real. This phenomenon was extensively documented by David Dunning and Justin Kruger in their famous 1999 study “Unskilled and Unaware of It.” They found that people with low ability in a domain often overestimate their competence because they lack the metacognitive skills to recognize their incompetence. This became known as the Dunning-Kruger effect. New thoughts and shifting perceptions began to surface, making me question what I was doing — and what was truly real. It made me do hard work, pushing my limits to what I was locked on, exploring the brilliant minds over the internet, and building connections with the inspirational profiles and experienced individuals. This is what I’ve discovered so far, and I hope to continue writing as I learn and explore the world around me. -Harsh Dhiman
